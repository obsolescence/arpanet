Overview

When the ARPANET moved out of its testbed in the early 1970s, NASA’s Ames Research Center was one of the government research labs tapped to join the experiment. Ames was an appealing ARPANET site: it combined heavy computational needs for aeronautics and simulation with an engineering culture comfortable with advanced computer systems. As such, Ames was one of the first government research centers to host an IMP and attach multiple powerful computers and TIP services to the network.

Ames’s IMP was the focal point for several different kinds of hosts: large scientific mainframes, time-sharing systems, experimental supercomputers, and Terminal Interface Processors (TIPs) that let many local terminals access the network without a dedicated host.

Host 16,0: 360/67

NASA Ames maintained IBM 360-class systems for heavy batch science and engineering jobs. The 360/67 family was popular at government labs because it supported virtual memory and timesharing variants — useful for both scheduled large simulation runs and interactive research sessions. Typical uses at Ames included aerodynamic simulations, structural analysis, and engineering toolchains such as NASTRAN. These mainframes often acted as the “official” data-processing centers for large projects; they ran heavy FORTRAN jobs, handled tape libraries, and stored large experiment datasets that collaborators could request over the ARPANET.e corrupti.

Host 16,2: TIP

Ames ran Terminal Interface Processors (TIPs) to let many ASCII terminals (from engineering consoles to remote collaborators) connect to the ARPANET without each terminal needing its own host. TIPs were an essential way that ARPANET spread beyond the handful of large computers to actual human users on terminals across a lab — greatly multiplying who could use the network for email, file transfer and remote login. Ames’s ARPANET entry in 1973 directories explicitly list TIP connections.

People & Anecdotes

Ames’s aviation work required massive simulations. Large FORTRAN jobs ran on the 360 family; results were exchanged with other universities by file transfer. Structural and systems analysis (NASTRAN) simulations ran as queued jobs on the mainframes; ARPANET was used to submit jobs remotely and fetch results. Interactive research & software development was done on the TENEX/PDP-10 hostas well as human–computer interaction experiments and interactive data analysis. These machines were the social centers where algorithms and tools were developed and shared.

ACM Digital Library

Ames researchers could pull telemetry, datasets, documentation, and code from other labs (and receive mail) — functionally the same roles ARPANET played at other research sites. The TIPs made the network accessible to non-programmer researchers and engineers on terminals across Ames.

People

Ames’s ARPANET life was run by a mix of groups: NASA scientific staff, central computing facility personnel, systems programmers, and student or contractor engineers assigned to specific projects. Contemporary ARPANET participant lists and RFC distributions name network liaisons and contacts tied to Ames (for example, figures listed as contacts in RFC-era distribution lists), showing the institutional organization behind network participation. These liaisons bridged the technical needs of aeronautics projects with ARPA’s networking requirements.

Because Ames combined government lab discipline with a research-lab attitude, the culture around the computers tended to be a mix of careful systems engineering and hands-on curiosity: staff expected heavy, scheduled simulations to run cleanly, but they also encouraged experiments on network protocols and interactive tools that pushed the state of the art.

Anecdotes & human moments



The presence of large specialized supercomputers at Ames meant the ARPANET occasionally moved truly massive datasets for the time — returning results from CFD runs that would have been impossible to ship on tape quickly. Those transfers often tested both human patience and the network’s patience; scheduling became an art.

The TIP as a social portal

Engineers and visitors used TIP-attached terminals to exchange informal email, quick notes, and early mail group coordination. The TIPs were actually where non-programmers first saw the power of the network.
