Overview

In the early 1970s, when the ARPANET was still an experiment rather than an infrastructure, Stanford Research Institute (SRI) in Menlo Park served as one of the network’s most important and influential sites. Known officially in the network tables as SRI-ARC (the Augmentation Research Center) and SRI-AI (the Artificial Intelligence Center), the SRI node housed some of the most advanced computing research in the world and played a central role in shaping what would eventually become the Internet.

SRI-ARC Information
SRI’s IMP (Interface Message Processor) was among the earliest deployed—connected in 1969 as one of the first four ARPANET sites alongside UCLA, UCSB, and the University of Utah.
The IMP at SRI typically sat in the machine room near the SDS 940, humming beside the refrigerator-sized computers and racks of modem lines.
The IMP connected SRI to the growing national network via a 50 kbps line, and from that single blue Honeywell box branched out the local interfaces to the center’s internal machines.

Host 2,0: PDP-10

The star of the SRI node was the SDS 940, a time-sharing mainframe that ran Doug Engelbart’s revolutionary NLS (oN-Line System).
It was used for:
- interactive text editing
- hypertext linking
- screen windows
- remote shared editing
-collaborative conferencing (Journal and Dialogue systems)
- early email-like messaging
- project documentation systems

This single machine played an outsized historical role. It was the host machine for the famous 1968 “Mother of All Demos”, where Engelbart and his team demonstrated the mouse, hypertext, shared screens, videoconferencing, and real-time collaboration — long before they existed elsewhere.

By the time NLS was on the ARPANET, remote researchers could log in from any other ARPANET terminal and use the system just as local ARC researchers did.

Host 2,1: PDP-15

SRI also operated PDP-10 machines at the SRI AI Center (SAIL) and at ARC:
- Used for early artificial intelligence research
- Natural-language systems
- Early robotics work
- Symbolic computation
- Route-planning research (which later influenced navigation systems)

The PDP-10s communicated with the IMP via host interface hardware designed in collaboration with BBN engineers.

PDP-10 systems at SRI were well known for running experimental operating systems and knowledge-engineering tools. SRI’s AI researchers were among the heaviest ARPANET users, frequently logging into remote sites for collaboration.

People & Anecdotes

People and Culture


SRI’s node was defined as much by the people who worked around it as by the machines themselves. Two organizational centers were particularly central: the Augmentation Research Center (ARC) led by Doug Engelbart, and the SRI AI programs.


Doug Engelbart and the ARC


Doug Engelbart and his ARC team were pioneers of collaborative computing. Engelbart’s long‑running project aimed to “augment human intellect” through tools that supported shared work, linking, and real‑time interaction. The NLS system, the mouse, and the experimental collaboration tools emerged from this mindset.


The ARC team included engineers and researchers who later influenced graphical user interfaces, hypertext systems, and collaborative software. Their work exemplified a purposeful, mission‑driven lab culture in which technology served to extend human cognitive capacity.



The AI Center and Robotics Researchers


SRI’s AI researchers — working on robotics, planning, and language understanding — used the ARPANET to share code, data, and results with remote collaborators. Their experimental code and datasets often crossed the network, speeding the pace of discovery.



Anecdotes and Notable Moments



The First Message


When the ARPANET first linked multiple sites, the earliest exchange produced the humble string “lo.” The system had crashed after transmission began, so the first transmitted characters were literally “lo,” a small but memorable beginning for what would become global communications.


The NLS Demo Room as a Hub


Following Engelbart’s 1968 demonstrations, the NLS console and demo space became a proto‑teleconferencing center. Remote researchers could log into the SDS 940 and witness edits and link manipulations in real time — an early incarnation of collaborative editing and remote meetings.


Robotics and Remote Computing


SRI’s robotics projects occasionally transferred control code and datasets across the ARPANET. Researchers joked that the robots were among the earliest 'network‑aware' devices, downloading software and directives over the same lines that connected human researchers.
